What the MN Mapper Measures

(formerly MNTEST)

Two dimensions: the Nine Natures and the Ten Intelligences. What scores represent, what they do not, and why the difference between preference and capacity matters.

The MN Mapper measures two distinct dimensions. The first is Natures—patterns in where engagement flows easily and where it costs more. The second is Intelligences—patterns in how you process, learn, and express. These are related but not the same.

Understanding what each dimension captures—and what it does not—is essential to reading results accurately. Scores are not rankings. They are not measures of ability. They are not predictions. They are patterns in self-reported preference that point toward where to look.

The Nine Natures dimension

The Natures dimension measures where engagement flows and where it costs. It asks: when you supply this kind of engagement, does it sustain you or deplete you?

The nine Natures are:

Protective — engagement with vigilance, accuracy, boundary-holding, prevention of error. Attention to what could go wrong and ensuring it doesn't.

Educative — engagement with explaining, clarifying, helping others understand. The work of translating knowledge into comprehension.

Administrative — engagement with organization, sequencing, follow-through, maintenance. Keeping systems running and processes on track.

Creative — engagement with idea generation, originality, new combinations. Making something that wasn't there before.

Healing — engagement with care, repair, presence with difficulty. Being with what is broken or hurting.

Entertaining — engagement with energy, performance, atmosphere-shaping. Bringing life and vitality to spaces.

Providing — engagement with service, support, meeting others' needs. Ensuring others have what they require.

Entrepreneurial — engagement with initiative, ownership, building something new. Taking responsibility for making something happen.

Adventurous — engagement with risk, novelty, movement toward uncertainty. Seeking what is unknown or unexplored.

Everyone can supply all nine. The test measures where your supply flows easily—where the work feeds rather than depletes you—and where supply carries more cost.

The Ten Intelligences dimension

The Intelligences dimension measures how you process, learn, and express. It asks: what channels work best for you? How do you naturally take in and work with information?

The ten Intelligences are:

Gross Bodily — capacity with whole-body coordination, large muscle movement, physical presence in space.

Fine Bodily — capacity with precise motor control, detailed hand work, manipulation of small objects.

Interpersonal — capacity with understanding others, reading social dynamics, navigating relationships.

Intrapersonal — capacity with self-understanding, internal awareness, knowing your own patterns.

Logical — capacity with reasoning, pattern recognition, systematic thinking.

Linguistic — capacity with words, language, verbal and written expression.

Graphic Visual — capacity with images, 2D representation, visual design and composition.

Spatial Visual — capacity with 3D space, navigation, mental rotation and spatial reasoning.

Musical — capacity with rhythm, tone, melody, and sound patterns.

Naturalistic — capacity with recognizing and categorizing patterns in the natural world.

These describe channels of processing. Higher scores indicate stronger preference for working through that channel. They do not measure talent or guarantee performance.

What scores represent

Scores represent self-reported patterns. They show where you indicated preference consistently across multiple questions. Higher scores mean you responded in ways that suggest that nature or intelligence resonates more. Lower scores mean less resonance.

Scores are relative to your other scores. A high score in one area means that area showed more preference than your other areas. It does not mean you have "a lot" of that nature or intelligence in some absolute sense. It means that, among the options, that one stood out.

Scores are patterns, not points. A score of 7 versus 8 is not a meaningful distinction. What matters is the shape—which areas cluster higher, which cluster lower, which stand out from the rest.

Scores are starting points. They point toward where to look, not where to conclude. A high score says "observe this more closely." A low score says "this may not be where your energy flows, but watch and see."

What scores do not represent

Scores are not ability. A high score in an intelligence does not mean you are good at it. A low score does not mean you are bad at it. The test measures preference—what channels you gravitate toward—not performance or skill.

Scores are not identity. A high Protective score does not make you "a Protective person." It suggests that Protective engagement may flow more easily for you. You are not your scores. You are someone with patterns, and patterns can shift.

Scores are not prescription. High scores do not tell you what to do. They do not say "pursue this career" or "avoid this role." They provide orientation—information about where engagement tends to flow—not direction.

Scores are not fixed. Patterns can shift as contexts change, as you develop, as life unfolds. What flows easily now may cost more later. What costs now may become easier. The scores capture a moment, not a permanent state.

Scores are not comprehensive. The test captures self-reported patterns on two dimensions. It does not capture everything about you. Other frameworks, other observations, other knowledge all have their place. The MN Mapper is one lens, not the only lens.

Preference versus capacity

This distinction is central to understanding what the MN Mapper measures. Preference is what you are drawn to. Capacity is what you can do. These are not the same.

You may have high capacity in something that depletes you. Many people excel at work that drains them. They have the ability—the skill, the talent, the training—but supplying that engagement costs more than it gives. Capacity without preference leads to competent exhaustion.

You may have preference for something where your capacity is still developing. You are drawn to it, it sustains you, but you are not yet skilled. Preference without capacity leads to energized learning—the work of getting better at something that feeds you.

The MN Mapper measures preference. It asks where engagement flows easily, not where performance is strong. A high score means that kind of engagement tends to sustain you. It does not mean you are skilled at it. Skill is a different question.

This is why the Natures and Intelligences dimensions work together but measure different things. Intelligences are closer to capacity—how you can process and work. Natures are closer to cost—what it takes to keep working that way. Both matter. Neither alone is complete.

Why this matters

When preference and capacity are confused, people are praised for what drains them. "You're so good at this" becomes pressure to keep doing something that costs. Ability becomes obligation.

When scores are read as identity, people become their results. "I'm an Adventurous type" closes off observation and locks patterns in place. What was meant to be a starting point becomes a conclusion.

When scores are read as prescription, they become another voice telling people what to do. "Your results say you should..." replaces agency with compliance. What was meant to return choice takes it away.

Understanding what scores actually represent—self-reported patterns in preference, starting points for observation, hypotheses to test—prevents these errors. It keeps the test in its proper place: useful but limited, informative but not definitive.
