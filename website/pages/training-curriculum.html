What You'll Learn

MN Training teaches the framework deeply. It also teaches what the framework cannot do. Learning limits is not separate from learning MN. It is the core competence.

The curriculum covers everything needed to use MN accurately in professional contexts. This includes the conceptual framework, the assessment tool, and the practical skills of observation and communication.

But equally important is what you learn not to do. MN Training spends significant time on the boundaries of the framework—where it stops, what it cannot say, and how to recognize when you are drifting beyond observation into prescription.

The ability to stop well is the mark of competent MN practice.

Deep understanding of the MN framework

You will learn the theoretical foundations of Multiple Natures. This includes the core model: how the Nine Natures describe patterns of intrinsic engagement—what draws a person, what they naturally move toward when nothing is pushing or pulling.

You will learn the demand/supply/cost model that distinguishes MN from personality typing. MN does not describe who someone is. It describes how activities make demands, how people supply responses, and what those responses cost.

You will learn how the framework emerged from Howard Gardner's Multiple Intelligences and how it extends that work in a specific direction—from capacity to engagement, from what someone can do to what draws them.

The Nine Natures

You will learn each of the Nine Natures in detail. Not as labels to apply to people, but as lenses for observing patterns of engagement.

For each Nature, you will understand what it responds to, what kinds of activities make demands that align with it, and what it looks like when someone is operating from that Nature versus performing it at cost.

You will learn to see Natures in action—not to categorize people, but to recognize patterns as they appear in real contexts.

The Ten Intelligences

You will learn the Ten Intelligences that MN uses—Gardner's original framework extended and adapted for this context.

You will understand how Intelligences and Natures relate: Intelligences describe capacities; Natures describe engagement. Someone may have high capacity in an area without being drawn to it. Someone may be drawn to an area without high capacity. The relationship between what you can do and what draws you is not automatic.

You will learn to distinguish between these dimensions and to communicate about them clearly.

Using the MN Mapper accurately

You will learn how to use the MN Mapper—the assessment tool that helps people see their own patterns of engagement.

This includes the mechanics: how to administer the test, what the questions measure, how scores are calculated. But more importantly, it includes interpretation: what the results can and cannot tell you.

The MN Mapper produces data about self-reported patterns. It does not produce predictions, prescriptions, or career recommendations. You will learn to interpret results without crossing into claims the data cannot support.

Interpreting results without prescribing

A significant portion of training focuses on the skill of interpretation without prescription. This is harder than it sounds.

When someone sees their MN Mapper results, there is a strong pull—both for them and for you—to make meaning. To say what it means for their life, their career, their relationships. To give advice.

MN Training teaches you to resist that pull. To describe what you see without adding what they should do about it. To offer observation and let them make meaning for themselves.

The goal is not to be unhelpful. The goal is to be accurate. Prescription dressed as observation is still prescription. Learning to stop at observation is the skill.

Recognizing drift and overclaim

You will learn to recognize when you are drifting from observation into territory MN does not support.

Drift happens gradually. It starts with accurate observation, then adds interpretation, then adds implication, then adds advice. Each step feels natural. The skill is noticing when you have crossed from what you can see to what you are adding.

Overclaim is the result of unnoticed drift. Saying MN shows someone should pursue a certain career. Saying a low score means they cannot do something. Saying their Natures explain their relationship difficulties. None of these claims are supported by MN. Learning to catch yourself before you make them is essential.

Working within limits

MN has specific limits, and training teaches you to work within them.

MN does not predict behavior. It does not prescribe careers. It does not diagnose problems. It does not explain everything about a person. It does not determine fit for jobs, relationships, or life paths.

What MN does is offer a lens for observing patterns of engagement. That lens is valuable precisely because it stays in its lane. When it tries to do more, it becomes another typing system making claims it cannot support.

Training teaches you to value the limits as much as the capabilities. The limits are what make MN useful.

Maintaining agency for clients

You will learn to use MN in ways that maintain and enhance client agency rather than reducing it.

This means presenting results as information, not verdicts. It means inviting clients to respond to what they see rather than telling them what it means. It means treating their interpretation as primary and yours as secondary.

The client knows their life. You know the framework. The goal is to give them a tool they can use, not a reading they must accept.

What you learn NOT to do

Training explicitly covers what not to do. This is not a secondary concern. It is central to competent practice.

You learn not to type people. MN is not a typing system, and using it as one undermines both its accuracy and its value.

You learn not to prescribe. Observation stays observation. What someone should do with what they observe is their decision.

You learn not to predict. MN Mapper results do not tell you what someone will do, enjoy, or succeed at. They tell you patterns of reported engagement. That is different.

You learn not to explain. When clients want MN to explain their struggles, their relationships, their career difficulties, you learn to redirect to what MN can actually offer—a lens, not a diagnosis.

You learn not to sell certainty. Clients often want definitive answers. Learning to offer useful uncertainty without pretending to knowledge you don't have is a key skill.

Practical application

Training includes practice. You will work with the framework, interpret results, communicate about patterns, and receive feedback on where you drift.

The practice is designed to develop skill, not just knowledge. Knowing the limits intellectually is different from maintaining them under pressure, when a client is asking for more than MN can give. Practice builds the capacity to hold the line.

By the end of training, you should be able to use MN accurately in your professional context—whatever that context is. You should be able to recognize your own drift, correct it, and help clients get value from MN without overclaiming what it offers.
