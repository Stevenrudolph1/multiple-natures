Non-Affiliated Use Guidance

If you completed MN Training but are not currently affiliated, here is what you can and cannot say about your relationship to Multiple Natures.

This guidance is for professionals who completed MN Training but are not currently affiliated. This includes people who never affiliated after training, as well as people whose affiliation has lapsed.

The distinction between past training and current practice matters. It protects people seeking MN services and maintains honesty about what professional credentials mean.

This page clarifies appropriate language. The intent is helpful guidance, not policing.

What non-affiliates can say

If you completed training, you can speak about that training in past tense. These statements are accurate and appropriate:

"I completed MN certification in [year]."

"I was certified in Multiple Natures."

"I trained in MN with Steven Rudolph."

"I completed the MN Training program."

"My work is informed by Multiple Natures."

"I draw on MN concepts in my practice."

"I studied the Multiple Natures framework."

These statements describe what happened. They do not claim current practitioner status. They are honest about the relationship.

What non-affiliates cannot say

Without current affiliation, present-tense practitioner language is not appropriate. These statements require affiliation:

"I am an MN Practitioner."

"I practice Multiple Natures."

"I am certified in MN." (present tense implies current status)

"I offer MN-based coaching."

"I use MN with my clients."

"MN Practitioner" (as a title or credential)

These statements claim current practice. They imply an ongoing relationship that affiliation provides. Without affiliation, they misrepresent your status.

Examples in context

On a website bio:

Appropriate: "Sarah completed MN certification in 2022 and draws on Multiple Natures concepts in her coaching practice."

Not appropriate: "Sarah is an MN Practitioner offering MN-based coaching."

In conversation with a potential client:

Appropriate: "I trained in Multiple Natures and my approach is informed by that framework."

Not appropriate: "I'm an MN Practitioner. I practice Multiple Natures."

On professional materials:

Appropriate: "Trained in Multiple Natures (2021)"

Not appropriate: "MN Practitioner" or "Certified MN Professional"

The distinction explained

The test is grammatical. Past tense describes what happened. Present tense claims current status.

"I was certified" is past tense. It describes a historical event. That event happened and remains true.

"I am a practitioner" is present tense. It claims current status. That status requires current affiliation.

This is not a technicality. When someone says "I am an MN Practitioner," it should mean they are actively practicing with current standing. Anything else misleads people seeking services.

Why this matters

The distinction protects everyone involved.

For people seeking services: When they find someone claiming to be an MN Practitioner, they should know that person has current standing, not just historical training from years ago.

For trained professionals: Clear guidelines remove ambiguity. You know exactly what you can and cannot say.

For the framework: Multiple Natures is better served by practitioners who maintain an active relationship than by an ever-growing list of people who once trained.

If you're unsure

The grammatical test usually resolves uncertainty. Ask: Is this statement past tense or present tense?

Past tense statements about training are appropriate without affiliation.

Present tense statements about practice require affiliation.

If you have a specific question about appropriate language, contact us. We are happy to help clarify.

This is guidance, not enforcement

This page provides clarity on appropriate language. It is meant to help, not to threaten.

We trust that professionals who trained in MN understand why these distinctions matter and will represent their relationship to the framework honestly.

If you have been using language that does not fit these guidelines, now you know. The path forward is simple: adjust your language to reflect past training, or affiliate to enable present-tense usage.
