Training Format

MN Training runs as a live cohort with real-time sessions. The format is not self-paced. Learning to see accurately requires feedback in the moment.

The live cohort format is not incidental to MN Training—it is essential. The skills being developed require real-time feedback and correction. Self-paced learning cannot provide this.

When someone drifts from observation into prescription, they often don't notice it themselves. When someone uses typing language instead of pattern language, it sounds natural to them. These errors need correction in the moment, from someone who can hear what they are actually saying.

The cohort format also means you learn with others. You see their drift, which helps you recognize your own. You hear different applications of the framework, which deepens your understanding. The community is part of the pedagogy.

Why not self-paced

Self-paced learning works well for content that can be absorbed through reading and reflection. It does not work well for skills that require real-time feedback.

MN Training is primarily skill development, not content delivery. The content—the Nine Natures, the Ten Intelligences, the MN Mapper (formerly MNTEST)—can be described in writing. But learning to use them accurately requires practice with correction.

Consider: you read about the importance of staying with observation. You understand the principle. Then you practice interpreting someone's results, and without noticing, you slip into prescription. "With your high Creative Nature, you might consider careers in design." That sounds helpful. It also crosses a line MN does not support.

In a live session, this gets caught and corrected immediately. In self-paced learning, it gets reinforced through practice.

Why live cohorts

The live cohort format serves several purposes beyond real-time feedback.

Shared learning. Participants see each other work with the framework. They hear each other's questions, watch each other's practice, learn from each other's corrections. This multiplies the learning opportunities.

Community formation. By the end of training, participants know each other. They have worked together, struggled together, corrected each other. This creates a foundation for ongoing professional community.

Commitment structure. Scheduled sessions create accountability. The cohort moves forward together. This supports completion in a way that self-paced formats often do not.

Real-time modeling. Participants see the framework being used accurately in real time. They see how to handle questions, how to redirect drift, how to maintain limits under pressure. This modeling is difficult to provide in recorded content.

Session structure

Training sessions combine several elements.

Conceptual teaching. Direct instruction in the framework, its components, and its limits. This is the content portion—what MN is, how it works, what it can and cannot do.

Demonstration. Live examples of MN being used—interpreting results, communicating about patterns, handling questions. You see the framework in action before you practice it yourself.

Practice. Participants work with the framework—interpreting results, describing patterns, responding to scenarios. This is where skill develops.

Feedback. Real-time correction during practice. When drift happens, it gets named. When overclaim appears, it gets caught. This is the learning that cannot happen asynchronously.

Discussion. Questions, clarifications, deeper exploration of difficult areas. The live format allows questions to go where they need to go.

Between-session work

Training is not only the live sessions. There is work between sessions that prepares you for the next phase and deepens what was covered.

This includes reading and reflection. Some content is better absorbed through reading than through live instruction. Materials are provided for this purpose.

It includes practice assignments. You work with the framework on your own—interpreting results, preparing responses, identifying drift in examples. This practice feeds into the live sessions.

It includes observation. You notice MN-relevant patterns in your own work, in conversations, in the people around you. This informal practice develops the observational skill that MN requires.

The between-session work is not optional. It is part of training. Participants who skip it arrive less prepared for the live sessions and learn less from them.

Cohort size

Cohorts are kept small enough to allow real interaction. Every participant should have opportunity to practice, to receive feedback, to ask questions.

Large cohorts compromise this. If there are too many participants, individual practice time shrinks. Feedback becomes less personal. Questions get less attention. The benefits of the live format diminish.

Specific cohort sizes are listed with each scheduled training. When a cohort fills, we open another rather than expanding beyond effective size.

Technology requirements

Training sessions are conducted via video conference. You will need:

A stable internet connection capable of video calls.

A computer or device with camera and microphone. Video participation is expected—this is not a format where you listen passively.

A quiet environment where you can participate fully. The interactive nature of sessions requires your active presence.

Specific platform details and technical requirements are provided upon enrollment.

Time commitment

Training requires time commitment for both live sessions and between-session work.

Live sessions are scheduled in advance, and attendance is expected. Missing sessions means missing content that cannot be fully recovered through recordings or materials—the feedback and practice portions especially.

Between-session work requires additional time. The exact amount varies, but participants should expect meaningful work between each session, not token assignments.

Total time commitment depends on the specific training schedule. Details are provided with each cohort listing.

What is required to complete

Completing training requires:

Attendance. Participation in the scheduled live sessions. Limited absence accommodation may be possible; details are provided with enrollment.

Between-session work. Completion of assigned reading, practice, and reflection between sessions.

Demonstrated competence. Training is not merely attendance-based. You need to demonstrate that you can use MN accurately—that you understand the framework, can interpret results, and can maintain appropriate limits.

The goal is competence, not seat time. Participants who do the work and develop the skill complete training. Participants who attend but do not develop the skill may need additional support before completion.

What completion provides

Completing training gives you deep understanding of MN and skill in using it accurately. It also makes you eligible to apply for affiliation if you want to practice MN professionally.

Completion does not automatically confer affiliation. Training and affiliation are separate. Most people who complete training do affiliate, but the decision is separate and the process is separate.
