Certification vs. Affiliation

Certification is a historical training record. Affiliation is a current naming license. Understanding this distinction is essential.

The distinction between certification and affiliation is the governing principle of professional MN practice. It determines what you can say about your relationship to Multiple Natures—and when.

The distinction is grammatical at its core. Certification is past tense. Affiliation is present tense. This simple test clarifies nearly every question about usage.

What certification is

Certification is a historical training record. It documents that you completed MN Training at a specific time.

Certification is permanent. It is NEVER revoked. If you completed training in 2024, you completed training in 2024. That fact does not change and can never be taken away.

Certification gives you the right to speak about your training in past tense:

"I completed MN certification in 2024."
"I was certified in Multiple Natures."
"I trained in MN with Steven Rudolph."
"My work is informed by MN."

These statements describe what happened. They do not claim current practice.

What certification does not give you

Certification alone does not give you the right to:

Identify as a current practitioner. "I am an MN Practitioner" is present tense. It requires affiliation.

Use the MN Mapper (formerly MNTEST) professionally. Access to the MN Mapper for client use requires both affiliation and separate MN Mapper licensing.

Be listed in the Practitioner Directory. The directory lists current affiliates, not everyone who has ever been trained.

Use MN-branded language in present tense. "I practice Multiple Natures," "MN-based coaching," and similar phrases require affiliation.

What affiliation is

Affiliation is a current naming license. It grants permission to use the Multiple Natures name in the present tense.

Affiliation is time-bound. It runs for one year and must be renewed. When affiliation lapses, the right to use present-tense MN language lapses with it.

Affiliation gives you the right to speak about your practice in present tense:

"I am an MN Practitioner."
"I practice Multiple Natures."
"I offer MN-based coaching."
"I use MN in my work with clients."

These statements describe current practice. They require affiliation.

The grammatical test

When in doubt, apply the grammatical test:

Past tense → Certification is sufficient.
"I completed training." "I was certified." "I learned MN."

Present tense → Affiliation is required.
"I am a practitioner." "I practice MN." "I use MN with clients."

This test resolves most questions about what can be said and when.

The four possible states

A person can be in one of four states with respect to MN:

Certified and affiliated. Completed training and currently affiliated. Can use past-tense and present-tense language. Listed in directory. Eligible for MN Mapper licensing.

Certified but not affiliated. Completed training but affiliation has lapsed or was never established. Can use past-tense language only. Not listed in directory. Not eligible for MN Mapper licensing.

Affiliated but not certified. This state is generally not possible—affiliation typically requires prior training. Some historical exceptions may exist.

Neither certified nor affiliated. Has not completed training. Cannot use MN professional language in any tense.

Why this distinction exists

The distinction between certification and affiliation serves several purposes.

Clarity for the public. When someone identifies as an MN Practitioner, it means they are currently practicing—not that they once learned the framework years ago. This protects people seeking MN services.

Quality maintenance. The affiliation relationship is ongoing. Affiliates stay connected to the community, receive updates, and maintain their practice actively. This is different from a one-time credential.

Honesty about time. Professional practice is not a permanent state conferred by past training. It is something you do now. The language should reflect this.

No grandfather exceptions. Someone trained years ago has the same obligations as someone trained recently. Past training, however extensive, does not substitute for current affiliation.

The professional analogy

This structure follows standard professional practice.

A law degree is permanent, but bar membership requires annual renewal to practice law. A medical degree is permanent, but licensure requires maintenance to practice medicine. A coaching certification is permanent, but professional affiliation is often renewed annually.

Certification records competence. Affiliation grants current permission to represent.

This is not a technicality or a cash grab. It is how professional credentialing works—distinguishing between what you learned and what you are currently authorized to do.

What happens when affiliation lapses

If your affiliation lapses—whether by choice or by not completing renewal—several things change:

You can no longer use present-tense MN language. "I am an MN Practitioner" becomes inaccurate.

You are removed from the Practitioner Directory.

Your MN Mapper licensing (if you had it) becomes inactive.

Your certification remains intact. It is NEVER affected by affiliation lapse. "I completed MN training in [year]" remains accurate—that historical fact hasn't changed and can never be taken away.

If you want to restore present-tense usage, you apply for re-affiliation. The path back is clear. You do not need to retrain or recertify.

Examples

Correct (certified, not affiliated):
"I completed MN certification in 2022."
"My work is informed by Multiple Natures."
"I trained with Steven Rudolph."

Incorrect (certified, not affiliated):
"I am an MN Practitioner."
"I practice Multiple Natures."
"I offer MN-based services."

Correct (certified and affiliated):
All of the above—both past-tense and present-tense statements.
